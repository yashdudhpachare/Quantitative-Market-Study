Objective · what the study set out to prove

That a population of anonymous shoppers is really a handful of distinct people — and that finding them in the numbers lets you speak to each one differently.

Focus: statistical tools to surface purchase patterns, then a segmentation built on top of them. The first two slides set the frame; everything after is evidence.

Welch’s t-test Total Owed · by gender
Compare mean spend of two groups when variances differ.
t = 6.52, p = 7.6e−11. Men and women spend significantly differently.
Chi-Square Quantity × Gender
Test association between two categorical variables.
χ² = 34.2, p = 3.7e−5. A real link between gender and basket size.
Pearson r Age ↔ Total Owed
Measure linear relationship between two continuous variables.
r ≈ 0.004, p ≈ 0.69. Age does not predict spend — a key reason to cluster carefully.
Statistical Power 1 − β
Probability of catching a real effect when it exists.
Power = 1.0. With ~7,665 rows the tests are decisive, not marginal.

Where we’d take it next

A class project is a proof of method, not a finished model. Here’s how we’d harden the segmentation next.

01 · Scale the features
Standardize inputs and rethink what we cluster on.

Total Owed dwarfs Quantity and Age in raw units, so K-Means is dominated by spend. Z-scoring the features — and replacing Age (which the data showed is unrelated to spend) with a behavioural signal — would sharpen the segments.

02 · Validate the clusters
Confirm k with a silhouette score, not just the elbow.

The elbow suggests five, but it’s a judgement call. A silhouette analysis plus reported cluster sizes and centroids would make the five-segment claim defensible, not just plausible.

03 · Name the bias
Move toward a true RFM model on representative data.

The sample skews young and male because it was collected across a class. A Recency-Frequency-Monetary segmentation on a broader, representative dataset would let these strategies generalize beyond the cohort.
