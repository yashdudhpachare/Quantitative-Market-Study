I
01 · Collect & clean

We didn't ask the customers. We read their receipts.

The brief was targeted marketing built on evidence. It begins with raw purchase records — price, quantity, age, gender, geography — and the unglamorous work of turning them into something trustworthy.

Raw, the set held 7,865 orders. After trimming extreme outliers, 7,665 remained — a clean core every later test could trust.

II
02 · Explore

What the purchases were quietly saying

Before any test, the data was made to speak in pictures — counts, prices, correlations, seasonality and age. A few signals showed up again and again.

Two reads jump out: Daily Essentials and Fashion are over half of all orders — and Electronics sells least often yet carries the biggest basket.

Total Owed tracks Unit Price at r = 0.98. Quantity and age barely register — price, not volume, drives the total.

The turn

A chart can suggest. Only a test can decide.

Four hypothesis tests separate the patterns that are real from the ones the eye invents — is this difference genuine, or just what we wanted to see?

III
03 · Test

Where the eye ends, the test begins

Welch's t-test, Chi-Square, Pearson correlation and a power check — each asking a sharper question than a chart can, across all 7,665 cleaned orders.

Men spend significantly more than women (p = 7.6e⁻¹¹) and place roughly 2.4× the orders; a Chi-Square test confirms order quantity and gender are linked (χ² = 34.2).

Age does not predict spend (r ≈ 0). And with n in the thousands, statistical power ≈ 1.0 — we can trust the misses as much as the hits.

IV
04 · Cluster

Five customers hiding in one dataset

Orders were sorted into price tiers, then clustered on spend, quantity and age with K-Means. The elbow method picked the number of groups; the groups picked themselves.

Inertia falls steeply, then flattens out — and the study settles on k = 5, the point where extra clusters stop buying much structure.

0–2

Low spenders, older-medium, young-high — the spend spectrum, from minimal to impulse.

3

Bulk buyers — frequent, low-value orders in larger quantity.

4

Quiet, low-quantity buyers — thin engagement, worth nurturing.

V
05 · Activate

A different message for each of the five

Segmentation only matters if it changes what you do. Each cluster gets a tactic that traces straight back to a number — a correlation, a test, a centroid.

Price-led discounts for the cautious, bundles for the steady, loyalty for the high-spenders, membership for the bulk buyers, and a dedicated touch for the quiet few.

The outcome

Five segments — and a marketing play for each.

The real output isn't a chart, it's a decision: one tactic per cluster, each one traceable to a number — a correlation, a hypothesis test, a cluster centroid. From 7,665 cleaned orders to a strategy a team can act on.

What the data also flagged
Seasonality

A mid-year order peak

Order volume runs highest in July and August and dips to a February low. The deck surfaces the pattern but not its cause — a signal to investigate, not yet a conclusion.

Revenue

Price, not quantity, moves the total

Total Owed correlates with unit price at r = 0.98, while quantity and age sit near zero — revenue rides on what customers buy, not how much.

Gender

Men order — and spend — more

Men place roughly 2.4× the orders of women, and Welch's t-test puts their higher spend past doubt (p ≈ 7.6 × 10⁻¹¹).
